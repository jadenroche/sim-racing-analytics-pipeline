# Phase 3 SQL Insights

## Early Findings

- Average finishing position is about 6.3 across 113 race records.
- Lower incident counts strongly correlate with better finishing positions.
- Track-level averages need context because some races include different cars, classes, or series.
- Oschersleben may be misleading because it included a multi-class sports car race where Mazda placement was naturally lower against faster classes.
- Future analysis should segment results by car, series, class, and strength of field.

## Analyst Note

Raw averages are useful, but they can be misleading without context. Better insights come from comparing similar race conditions.


## Incidents -> Performance

Minimizing incidents is the most impactful factor in improving race results. Lower incident races consistently result in higher finishing positions, suggesting that clean and consistent driving naturally leads to better outcomes, especially in lower and mid-strength fields.

## Car/Class Adaption (Mazda Baseline)

Performance in the Global Mazda MX-5 Cup serves as a baseline for overall driving ability. Lower finishing positions in higher-performance classes such as GT4 indicate a need for additional adaptation to vehicle dynamics, rather than a decline in overall skill.

## Strategy by SOF

In lower strength-of-field races, consistent and clean driving is often sufficient to achieve strong finishes, as many competitors make avoidable errors. This suggests a strategy shift where risk-taking is reduced in favor of consistency, allowing positions to be gained naturally.

## Track + Experience Hypothesis

Performance varies by track, with stronger results observed at tracks such as Virginia International Raceway and Oulton Park. This may be influenced by track familiarity, layout characteristics, or race frequency, and warrants further analysis.

### Findings
    Underperformance varies by track type: some tracks such as Summit Point show high incident rates contributing to poor results, while others like Watkins Glen show lower incident rates but still weaker finishes, indicating pace and track familiarity as limiting factors.

    Tracks with lower average incident counts, such as VIR and Oulton Park, consistently produce stronger results, reinforcing the importance of control and consistency on track-specific performance.
